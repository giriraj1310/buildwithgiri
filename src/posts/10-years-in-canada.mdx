Ten years ago I landed in Canada because a friend was applying and I thought — he's doing it, I'll do it too.

That was the whole plan. No network. No family here. No real picture of what the next five years would look like. Just a backpack, a student visa, and the kind of optimism that comes from not yet knowing what you're walking into.

I'm writing this because it's been a decade, and I think there's something worth saying that I couldn't have said at year one, or even year five.

## The Basement and the Clean Slate

The first place I lived in Canada was a basement. That was also the first time I learned that basements existed as places people actually lived. It was five minutes from college, which was all that mattered.

What I remember most about those early days wasn't the culture shock or the cold. It was how freeing it felt to be completely unknown. Nobody had a version of me in their head. Nobody's expectations were already set. I was starting on a clean slate, and I found that genuinely exciting rather than terrifying.

That mindset carried me further than any skill I had at the time.

## My First Job, My Landlady, and a Pair of Slippers

Before I understood what a resume was, my landlady walked me to a nearby pizza store and introduced my situation to the rep at the counter — who turned out to be the owner. She gave me the job on the spot. The only thing she said to me was: "You should wear closed shoes." I'd shown up in slippers.

I worked there for about a year. Bought FIFA 17 with my first paycheck. That felt like a win.

What I didn't know then was how much that job was teaching me — showing up, being reliable, following through on small things. None of it felt like a career lesson at the time. It just felt like surviving.

(Before the pizza store, I'd worked one month at a convenience store. The owner never paid me. When I asked him about it, he told me I had to wait another year because I couldn't legally sell cigarettes until I was 19. I left.)

## The Summer That Changed Everything

After my third semester, I had a four-month summer break. I decided not to go back to India. I thought I could use the time to earn money and chip away at my tuition. Then I lost the pizza store job.

So I applied through a staffing agency — open to anything. They placed me in a factory. Six days a week. Twelve hours a day. 7AM to 7PM.

I'd take the bus before sunrise, come back late, figure out what to eat, and do it again. It was the hardest stretch of my life in Canada, and I won't romanticize it. I was depressed. There were days on that factory floor where I made a very specific promise to myself: *I will do whatever it takes to never be here again. I will get a job in my field.*

That promise changed how I approached everything after.

(On a lighter note — when I first took the bus in Canada, I had no idea I had to pull a lever to signal my stop. I thought it stopped at every station automatically. I missed my stop.)

## The Macroeconomics Exam That Opened Something

Third semester, I picked macroeconomics as an elective without realizing microeconomics was a prerequisite. Failed the midterm. I was stressed in a way that surprised me — I hadn't expected an elective to throw me off more than my core courses.

But I turned it around. Scored 90+ on the final.

Something shifted in me after that. I'd always thought of myself as a technical person. That semester cracked open the business side of my brain, and it's been part of how I think ever since.

## IBM, and the Manager Who Taught Me to Show Up on Time

After the factory summer, I came back to college and committed to doing whatever it took to get a co-op in my field. Eventually, I landed at IBM.

It was one of the best professional experiences of my life.

My manager there was someone I genuinely learned from. Early on, I was late sometimes. He didn't make it a big thing — he just made it clear, calmly and directly, that being on time was one of the most important things you could do professionally. That lesson stuck with me in a way that nothing in a classroom ever has.

I went back to IBM for a second co-op. And a third — in technical support for Cognos Analytics.

After the third one, I had a conversation with my manager about a full-time role. He said yes. Then he handed me a blank piece of paper and asked me to write my salary expectation on it.

I wrote a number I thought was probably too high.

He came back a few minutes later. They'd accepted it.

I was happy, shocked, and completely over the moon. I still had a sixth semester to complete to finish my program, but the destination was set.

## The Decision I'm Most Proud Of (That Made Some People Upset)

One of the most deliberate choices I made early in Canada was not staying within my ethnicity.

I know that might read as a strange thing to be proud of. But I made that choice consciously — I believed the best way to learn, grow, and build something real was through a diverse group of people. And it was one of the best decisions I've ever made.

It came with backlash. Some people within my community were upset about it. I got comments. People talked.

But I grew up attending a private ISC board school in India, and that experience gave me a broader perspective than what I often encountered in those circles. I never quite fit the expected mould, and I stopped trying to.

The friendships I built across different backgrounds, cultures, and industries shaped how I think more than almost anything else.

## Tesla on a Friday

In 2020, about six months into my full-time role at IBM, I was helping friends move a mattress in a U-Haul. A Tesla drove past.

I said out loud: "I need a Tesla."

My friend laughed it off.

That same Friday at 3PM, we went to the Tesla showroom. I took a test drive. I booked it.

Monday: I had my Tesla.

I'm not sharing this to be flashy. I'm sharing it because that moment was a marker for me — a reminder of how far I'd come from the factory floor and the pizza store. Sometimes you need to do something that makes the distance visible.

## Oracle, Negotiation, and Getting Laid Off

A LinkedIn recruiter reached out about Oracle. I was skeptical — honestly thought it might be a scam. I took the leap anyway.

It was the first time I negotiated my salary. They came in low. I pushed back. I walked away with about $20,000 more than their initial offer.

The role was in the utilities sector — Oracle CC&B — and I had zero background in utilities. I understood the tech. But consulting requires business understanding, and that took time to build. I struggled. I kept the job because it paid well and gave me room to invest in other things.

Then in May 2024, I got laid off.

It hit hard. I felt like a failure for about a week. And then something shifted. I had time, minimal expenses, and EI. I started to see it as space rather than loss.

A referral to Adobe came shortly after. The role was a natural fit — close enough to what I'd done at IBM that it felt familiar, with enough new scope to stay interesting.

## What Ten Years Actually Taught Me

I've never been to the top of the CN Tower. Never taken the ferry at Niagara Falls. Visited the falls, but not the ferry. People find that strange.

I've been intentional about my time in a way that might look like missing out from the outside. About 80% of my time over the past decade has gone toward improving, learning, and finding the right people. The other 20% has been boys trips, camping, the things that actually refuel you.

Finding your people is genuinely one of the hardest things about living in a new country. You can be surrounded by people and still feel like none of them are quite your kind of people. You have to go out and find them actively — they rarely show up on their own.

A few things I'd tell someone landing today:

- **The clean slate is a gift. Use it.** Don't rush to recreate the social dynamics you left behind.
- **The uncomfortable jobs teach you things the good jobs can't.** Pay attention to what they're teaching you.
- **Negotiate. Always.** The first number they give you is rarely the final number.
- **Find one person further along the path who will actually talk to you.** One is enough to start.
- **Trust your gut more than you think you should.** Especially about people.

I came here because a friend was applying and I thought — why not me too?

Turns out that was the right question to ask.
