I've reviewed a lot of student portfolios. Most of them make the same mistakes. Not because the students aren't capable — but because they're optimizing for the wrong things.

## The Projects Nobody Cares About

Let me save you some time. Hiring managers have seen thousands of these:

- **To-do list apps** — unless yours has something genuinely novel about it
- **Weather apps** — same
- **Clone of [popular app]** — only impressive if you built something *on top* of it, not just replicated the UI

These aren't bad projects for *learning*. But they're not portfolio projects. A portfolio project answers the question: *"Why would someone pay you to build software?"*

## What Actually Works

The best student portfolios I've seen have one thing in common: the projects solve a real problem, even a small one.

Some examples of student projects that actually got interviews:

- A browser extension that flags potential phishing links in email clients
- A CLI tool that auto-formats research paper citations in APA/MLA
- A script that scraped Canadian immigration processing times and sent Discord alerts when wait times changed
- A dashboard that visualized a university's publicly available course enrollment data

None of these are technically complex. All of them are *specific*. You can immediately understand what they do and why someone would want them.

## The README Is Part of the Portfolio

Your code might be clean and your architecture might be smart — but if your README is three lines, none of that matters to a hiring manager who has 90 seconds per application.

A good portfolio project README includes:

```markdown
# Project Name

One-sentence description of what it does and who it's for.

## The Problem It Solves
[1–3 sentences. Why did you build this?]

## Tech Stack
[Brief. Don't list every dependency, just the meaningful choices.]

## What I Learned / What I'd Do Differently
[This is actually the most interesting section for interviewers.]

## Setup
[Make it work in under 5 minutes. If setup is hard, it's harder to evaluate.]
```

The "what I'd do differently" section is one most students skip. It's also one of the best signals of engineering maturity. Knowing what's wrong with your own code — and being able to articulate it — is a skill senior engineers value enormously.

## How Many Projects Do You Need?

One great project beats five mediocre ones. Full stop.

If you have one project you can speak to deeply — that you can explain from the initial problem through to specific technical decisions you made and why — that's enough for an entry-level interview.

Two is better. Three is plenty. More than that and you either didn't build them deeply or you're spreading too thin.

## The Portfolio Site Itself

Honestly? Keep it simple. A clean, fast-loading site with readable typography and working links beats an over-engineered showpiece that takes 4 seconds to load and has three broken pages.

The meta-lesson here: as a web developer, your portfolio site is itself a signal about your judgment. If it's slow, if it has a console full of errors, if the mobile view is broken — that's what hiring managers see.

A few things worth investing in:
- Lighthouse score above 90 (run it, fix the easy wins)
- Works on mobile (test it on your actual phone, not just the browser emulator)
- No broken links
- Contact method that actually works

## One More Thing

Add your portfolio link to LinkedIn. Put it in your email signature. Share the projects on Twitter/X when you ship something. You'd be surprised how many people get their first interview from someone who happened to see a project posted at the right time.

The portfolio doesn't find opportunities on its own. You still have to put it in front of people.

But when you do — make sure it's worth their time.
